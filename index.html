Prologue
The 175-Camera Illusion

Designing for the Lens

The stadium was a soundstage. Every angle, every prop, every human body was positioned not for the 68,000 in the stands — but for the 128 million at home watching through the lens of a Sony VENICE 2 or BURANO cinema camera.

Creative Direction

Human Engineering

Creative director Harriet Cuddeford and Yellow Studio faced the NFL's hard cap: no more than 25 motorized carts or large props on the field. Their solution? 380 human beings, each costumed as a blade of sugarcane — representing Puerto Rico's main cash crop under early U.S. territorial rule — who could rapidly walk off the field without a single machine.

The lush pastoral fields that millions saw at home? Pure human choreography. The live crowd saw something entirely different.

Chapter One
El Apagón · The Blackout

The Digital Classroom

When the lights flickered on those sparking electrical poles, art became a digital classroom. Bad Bunny and his dancers — dressed as jíbaros (traditional Puerto Rican rural farmers) in pavas, brimmed straw hats woven from palm leaves — scaled the poles before an audience of 128 million people.

Infrastructure & Aftermath

A Searing Critique

The tableau was a direct confrontation with Puerto Rico's chronic power failures — the unreliable grid that predated Hurricane Maria, and the catastrophic blackouts that followed the 2017 storm's landfall.

By embedding this critique inside the most-watched pop spectacle on the planet, Bad Bunny bypassed every traditional media gatekeeper and transmitted a political indictment to living rooms across the globe.

Chapter Two — The Jersey

The "Ocasio 64" Ambiguity

Bad Bunny took the field wearing a custom cream Zara jersey reading OCASIO 64. In the new media ecosystem, a single garment transmitted two simultaneous messages to two different audiences — at exactly the same time.

To the family network: a tribute to his late uncle Cutito, born in 1964, a devoted 49ers fan who never got to see his nephew on this stage.

To the political network: a stark reminder that the Puerto Rican government initially reported only 64 deaths from Hurricane Maria — a figure that independent researchers would later revise to over 3,000. The disputed number sparked nationwide protests where shoes were placed outside government buildings.

"In the new media reality, the network debated both — simultaneously, and without resolution."

Chapter Three
La Casita · The Diaspora

The Field Became a Narrative Landscape

The emotional centre of the stage was La Casita — a salmon-pink house modelled after a real home in Humacao, Puerto Rico. It was a direct nod to the pari de marquesina: the informal house parties that are a cornerstone of Puerto Rican social life.

Alongside it stood a Nuyorican barber shop, and behind the bar stood María Antonia "Toñita" Cay — the legendary owner of a Caribbean social club in Williamsburg, Brooklyn — pouring Bad Bunny a shot. The gesture collapsed the distance between the island and its New York diaspora into a single, wordless image.

Chapter Four
Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawái

A Warning, Not an Anthem

Everyone expected Livin' la Vida Loca. Instead, Ricky Martin delivered an excerpt from Bad Bunny's "Lo Que Le Pasó a Hawái" — a song that frames Hawaii as a cautionary tale for Puerto Rico about the consequences of over-tourism, capitalism, and gentrification.

The choice was a deliberate rejection of the expected crossover moment — and a demand for a different kind of attention.

The Desperate Plea

"Don't Let Go of the Flag"

Martin's performance urged listeners not to surrender the lelolai — a traditional Puerto Rican musical expression — framing the song as a last line of defence against deliberate whitewashing and cultural erasure.

Hawaii, once an independent kingdom absorbed into the United States, serves as the mirror: a paradise sold, a culture diluted, an identity nearly overwritten. The message to Puerto Rico was visceral and specific: you are watching it happen.

A pop song became a political instrument — transmitted to 128 million people through the most commercial event in American television.

Chapter Five
The Azul Celeste

Colors Carry History

The Puerto Rican flag that waved during the performance was not quite the official one. Its triangle was not the modern dark navy blue — it was azul celeste, a pale sky blue.

The distinction is not cosmetic. It is criminal history.

The light blue flag is the original banner of the Puerto Rican independence movement. Under the Gag Law (Law 53 of 1948) — the Ley de la Mordaza — displaying this flag was a criminal offence punishable by imprisonment. The law remained in force from 1948 until 1957.

By waving it on the Super Bowl stage in 2026, Bad Bunny transformed a 75-year-old act of civil disobedience into a live global broadcast. The 128 million who watched were — knowingly or not — witnesses to a flag that was once forbidden.

The Two Flags
1895

Azul celeste — the original independence flag. Criminalized under the Gag Law, 1948–1957. Worn on the Super Bowl stage, 2026.

2024

Official Puerto Rico flag — dark navy triangle. The azul celeste vs. navy distinction is a quiet, persistent marker of political identity.

Redefining the Word

A Continent,
Not a Country

The word América — in Spanish — refers to an entire hemisphere: North, Central, and South. The United States' claim on the word as a synonym for itself alone is, from this vantage point, an act of cultural erasure.

Bad Bunny's spike was a correction. A reclamation. Broadcast live to 128 million people.

While 68,000 sat inside Levi's Stadium, the real audience was everywhere else. They watched through 175 Sony cinema cameras that turned a football field into a slice of Puerto Rico coded purely for the lens.

The Opening Sequence
Prologue
Creative Direction

Human Engineering

Creative director Harriet Cuddeford and Yellow Studio faced the NFL's hard cap: no more than 25 motorized carts. Their solution? 380 human beings costumed as sugarcane, capable of walking off the field instantly, replacing machines with sheer manpower.

The Live Broadcast

Friction as Authenticity

Unlike Kendrick Lamar's hyper-precise, militarily choreographed 2025 halftime show, Bad Bunny's performance embraced friction. Early in the set, he battled noticeable mic issues, his raw vocals fighting against the stadium mix.

Yet, rather than derailing the spectacle, the technical flaws made the broadcast feel startlingly intimate and real. It stripped away the polished veneer of the NFL machine, turning the world's biggest stage into a massive, unpredictable block party.

Chapter One
El Apagón · The Blackout

The Digital Classroom

When the lights flickered on those sparking electrical poles, art became a digital classroom. Bad Bunny scaled the poles before an audience of 128 million people, bringing a searing critique of Puerto Rico's failing infrastructure straight to Middle America.

The sparking transformers were not just stage props—they were visceral symbols of the chronic blackouts plaguing the island's fragile, heavily privatized power grid since Hurricane Maria. The performance transformed daily infrastructural trauma into an unavoidable global spectacle.

The Viral "Dwerking" Moment
The "Safaera" Backlash

Queer Representation and Moral Panic

Bad Bunny performed the underground reggaeton anthem "Safaera." While he sang, two male dancers ground against each other on national television.

The moment triggered an immediate culture war. The outrage reinforced the show's dual reality: a celebration for the diaspora and a deliberate provocation to conservative America.

By centering unapologetic queer intimacy in the hyper-masculine arena of the NFL, Bad Bunny continued his career-long project of dismantling traditional machismo. The performance forced 128 million viewers to confront reggaeton's intersectional roots and the realities of queer Latinidad on the biggest stage possible.

Chapter Two: The Jersey

The "Ocasio 64"

Bad Bunny took the field wearing a custom cream Zara jersey reading OCASIO 64. In the new media ecosystem, a single garment transmitted two simultaneous messages.

To the family network: a tribute to his late uncle Cutito, born in 1964. To the political network: a stark reminder that the Puerto Rican government initially reported only 64 deaths from Hurricane Maria, a figure independent researchers revised to over 3,000.

Chapter Five
The Azul Celeste

Colors Carry History

The Puerto Rican flag that waved was not the modern dark navy blue; it was azul celeste, a pale sky blue. Under the Gag Law (Law 53 of 1948), displaying this specific flag was a criminal offence.

By waving the light blue flag, a 75-year-old act of civil disobedience was transformed into a live global broadcast. Compare it to the official navy flag adopted in 1952.

1895

Azul celeste: original independence flag. Criminalized 1948–1957.

1952

Dark Navy: the official Commonwealth flag, adopted to closely match the US flag.

The Hemisphere

Together,
We Are
America

As the performance reached its climax, a stadium-wide rally erupted. The flags of every nation across the Americas were unfurled, while Bad Bunny systematically named each country aloud, refusing to let any single nation dominate the identity of the continent.

The word América in Spanish refers to an entire hemisphere: North, Central, and South. The United States' claim on the word as a synonym for itself alone acts as a form of cultural erasure. Bad Bunny's final football spike was a correction. A reclamation. Broadcast live to 128 million people.
